# Introduction Writing Guide

## Goal

Write an introduction that leads naturally from disease problem to study design to the paper's central biological insight.

## Recommended Introduction Arc

1. Disease burden or clinical decision problem.
2. What current biomarkers, models, or mechanistic studies still fail to explain.
3. Why the missing piece requires a specific data resolution:
   - spatial context,
   - cell-state resolution,
   - multimodal integration,
   - longitudinal monitoring.
4. What this study did and why the design is appropriate.
5. Main contribution and expected impact.

## Paragraph Roles

Use 3-5 paragraphs with distinct jobs:

1. `Problem paragraph`: define the biological or clinical need.
2. `Gap paragraph`: explain why current studies or biomarkers are insufficient.
3. `Resolution paragraph`: justify why a multimodal or computational strategy is needed.
4. `This-study paragraph`: summarize cohorts, modalities, and core finding.
5. Optional `impact paragraph`: clarify translational significance.

## Writing Rules

1. Open with the disease question, not with software or algorithms.
2. Introduce modality choice as a response to the gap.
3. Name the central biological axis early when possible.
4. Do not over-explain pipeline internals in Introduction.
5. Ensure the final paragraph states both what was done and what was found.

## Good Lead-In Questions

1. What clinical outcome remains difficult to predict or improve?
2. What crucial context is missing from prior work?
3. Why can the target question not be answered by one modality alone?
4. What new layer of understanding does this study add?

## Checklist

1. Does each paragraph have one message?
2. Is the unmet need explicit by paragraph 1 or 2?
3. Is the rationale for multimodal design explicit?
4. Does the introduction already hint at the paper's central finding?
5. Are claims in the final paragraph aligned with Results evidence?
