# Results Writing Guide

## Goal

Write Results as a chain of biological answers, not as a chronology of analyses.

## Canonical Results Arc

Use this arc when applicable:

1. Overview of cohort and analysis workflow.
2. Discovery of the dominant molecular, spatial, or immune pattern.
3. Decomposition into cell types, gene programs, pathways, or interaction networks.
4. Orthogonal validation with another assay, pathology, independent cohort, or external dataset.
5. Clinical association:
   - prognosis,
   - therapy response,
   - stage progression,
   - risk group differences.
6. Mechanistic or translational implication.

## Subsection Title Rule

Prefer conclusion-like titles over procedure-like titles.

Good:

1. `Spatial transcriptomics identifies an immune barrier associated with immunotherapy resistance`
2. `High-risk tumors exhibit extracellular matrix remodeling and angiogenic signaling`
3. `Closer CD8 T-cell proximity to malignant cells predicts improved survival`

Weak:

1. `Spatial transcriptomics analysis`
2. `Single-cell analysis`
3. `Survival analysis`

## Paragraph Template

For each Results paragraph or paragraph pair:

1. State the question.
2. State the data and method briefly.
3. Report the key result with direction and contrast.
4. Explain the biological meaning.
5. Bridge to the next question.

## Statistic Reporting Rule

1. Always name the comparison groups.
2. Prefer effect direction plus magnitude over p-value alone.
3. Use cautious language for association.
4. Reserve stronger language only for evidence with mechanistic support.

## Validation Ladder

Stronger stories usually validate discoveries at more than one level:

1. same cohort, different modality,
2. independent cohort or external dataset,
3. functional experiment or perturbation,
4. histopathology or spatial confirmation.

## Checklist

1. Does each subsection answer one scientific question?
2. Does each title already reveal the local conclusion?
3. Is every major finding linked to a concrete modality or dataset?
4. Are discovery and validation clearly separated?
5. Does the section escalate toward biological or clinical meaning?
