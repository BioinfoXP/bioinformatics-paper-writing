# Bioinformatics Writing Patterns

## Purpose

Use this file when the paper involves multi-omics, spatial transcriptomics, tumor microenvironment, ligand-receptor analysis, biomarker discovery, prognostic modeling, or translational cancer biology.

## Patterns Extracted from the Source Papers

The source papers converge on several writing habits:

1. The paper story is anchored on one biological or clinical bottleneck.
2. The assay stack is introduced as a necessity, not a menu.
3. Results are written as a ladder:
   - detect a major pattern,
   - localize it to cell types or tissue regions,
   - explain it with interactions or molecular programs,
   - validate it with another modality or cohort,
   - connect it to prognosis, therapy response, or progression.
4. Subsection titles often already contain the conclusion.
5. Discussion explains why the multimodal synthesis changes biological understanding and what remains uncertain.

## Writing Implications

1. Start with the disease problem and unmet need before talking about algorithms.
2. Name cohorts, sample counts, and modality roles early.
3. Prefer verbs like `revealed`, `associated with`, `enriched`, `localized`, `validated`, and `suggested` unless stronger evidence exists.
4. Separate discovery from validation in both structure and wording.
5. Always answer:
   - What question did this analysis answer?
   - Why was this modality needed?
   - What was observed?
   - What does the observation imply biologically?

## Common Weak Patterns to Avoid

1. Writing a results paragraph as a methods log.
2. Mentioning many algorithms while hiding the biological conclusion.
3. Making a prognostic model sound clinically ready without external validation.
4. Claiming a mechanism from correlation alone.
5. Collapsing cell-state discovery, pathway enrichment, and survival association into one paragraph with no hierarchy.
