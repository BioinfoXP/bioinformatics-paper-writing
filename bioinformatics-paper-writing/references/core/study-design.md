# Study Design Guide

## Goal

Present the study design so readers immediately understand:

1. what biological or clinical question the paper asks,
2. which cohorts, tissues, or samples were used,
3. which modalities were applied and why each modality was necessary,
4. how discovery and validation are separated.

## Recommended Design Paragraph Order

1. Disease context and target question.
2. Discovery cohort and sample source.
3. Omics or pathology modalities and their role.
4. Validation resources:
   - independent cohort,
   - orthogonal assay,
   - external public dataset,
   - functional experiment.
5. Primary endpoints or target outputs.

## Modality Role Template

Use short, functional sentences:

1. `We used [modality] to identify [population-level, spatial, cellular, or proteomic] features of [target].`
2. `We used [second modality] to validate [signal or finding] in an orthogonal manner.`
3. `We used [clinical endpoint or model] to test whether these features were associated with [survival, response, or progression].`

## Discovery vs Validation Rule

Always mark which evidence belongs to:

1. discovery,
2. internal validation,
3. external validation,
4. mechanistic support.

Do not merge these categories into one vague phrase like `we further confirmed`.

## Checklist

1. Are cohort names, sample counts, and specimen types explicit?
2. Does each modality have a reason to exist in the story?
3. Is the distinction between association and mechanism explicit?
4. Can a reviewer see how the paper moves from observation to validation?
