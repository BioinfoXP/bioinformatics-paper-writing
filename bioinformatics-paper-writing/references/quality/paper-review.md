# Paper Review

## Goal

Use an adversarial, reviewer-style checklist to detect rejection risks early and revise the paper before submission.

## Critical Rule

Every major claim, especially in Abstract, Results, and Discussion, must be:

1. technically and biologically defensible, and
2. explicitly supported by cohort, assay, validation, or mechanistic evidence.

If a claim is not supported, either add evidence or weaken or remove the claim.

## What Usually Gets a Strong Paper Accepted

1. A meaningful biological or clinical insight.
2. A coherent multimodal or cohort design that justifies the claims.
3. Clear validation beyond the first discovery signal.
4. Appropriate caution about causality and clinical utility.

## Common Rejection Dimensions

1. Weak contribution:
   - descriptive signals without a memorable insight,
   - a model without enough biological or clinical meaning.
2. Over-interpretation:
   - correlation written as mechanism,
   - retrospective performance written as clinical readiness.
3. Incomplete validation:
   - no independent cohort,
   - no orthogonal assay,
   - no clear separation between discovery and validation.
4. Unclear study design:
   - sample counts, specimen types, or analysis flow are hard to reconstruct.
5. Weak writing logic:
   - Results read like a notebook dump,
   - subsection titles do not state findings.

## Self-Review Questions

### 1. Contribution

1. What new knowledge does this paper give to readers?
2. Is the biological or clinical problem meaningful?
3. Is there one central discovery or model that readers can remember?
4. Does the paper explain why the finding matters beyond this dataset?

### 2. Writing Clarity

1. Can a knowledgeable reader reconstruct the study design?
2. Does each paragraph have one message?
3. Are discovery and validation clearly separated?
4. Are terms for cell states, signatures, and cohorts consistent?

### 3. Evidence Strength

1. Is each main claim backed by the right assay or cohort?
2. Are effect direction and comparison groups always explicit?
3. Are the strongest claims supported by orthogonal evidence?
4. Are uncertainties reported honestly?

### 4. Validation Completeness

1. What is discovery only?
2. What is validated independently?
3. If there is a prognostic model, is external validation adequate?
4. If there is a mechanistic claim, is the evidence stronger than simple association?

### 5. Biological and Translational Soundness

1. Are clinical implications proportional to the evidence?
2. Is causal language used only when justified?
3. Could cohort bias, tissue heterogeneity, or confounding explain the result?
4. Does the paper state what additional evidence would be needed for translation?

## Reviewer Simulation Loop

Before finalizing, simulate these four voices:

1. Skeptical reviewer:
   - `What is the strongest overclaim here?`
2. Statistician:
   - `Is discovery separated from validation, and are comparisons explicit?`
3. Domain biologist:
   - `Does the interpretation really follow from the evidence?`
4. Translational reader:
   - `Is the claimed clinical importance appropriately scoped?`

Revise until the most likely objections are already answered in the text.
